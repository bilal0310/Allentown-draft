How the money works

The single most useful number on this page is the last one.

Squad9 playersYou + 8 signings
Budget200 ptsFixed, no trades
Minimum per player5 pts8 × 5 = 40 pts floor
Maximum on one player50 ptsHard cap — no exceptions
Truly spendable160 ptsAfter reserving the floor

No player can go for more than 50 points. That cap protects you from one ruinous bidding war, but it does not protect you from three expensive ones. Spend 50 twice and you have 60 points left for six slots — ten each, barely above the floor. That is the whole game.

Five rules for the table

Know your walk-away number before the name is called

The cap is 50, but 50 is a ceiling, not a suggestion. Decide your own number for each player while you're calm. Once bidding starts, the number you invented ten seconds ago is not a number, it's an emotion.

Let someone else win the first bidding war

The opening lots always go over value because everyone still feels rich. A captain who spends 60 in the first ten minutes is a captain playing the back half with pocket change.

Buy the second-best player in every category

The gap between the top name and the next one is usually small. The gap in price is not. That spread is where squads get built.

Count your slots out loud

Before every bid, say how many players you still need and how many points you have left. If points divided by slots drops under 10, stop bidding on anyone but bargains.

Points measure the past, not the day

Season totals reward whoever played the most matches. Check innings and strike rate, not just the headline number, and look at the Family Day column — some of these players are different people in a one-day format.
